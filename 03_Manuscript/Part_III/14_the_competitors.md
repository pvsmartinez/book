## Chapter 14: The Competitors

If you can run a six-minute mile, are you fast?

In a high school gym class, you are a god. At the Olympic trials, you don't make the cut. Your speed didn't change. The room did.

That's the third force in the Value Function: **The Competitors**.

### The Living Terrain

The Track is usually static. A mountain does not grow taller because you are a good climber. The rules of gravity don't change because you invented a better airplane.

Competitors are different. They are **Adaptive**. They are iterating at the same time you are, watching your successes, copying them, adjusting. In a system driven by The Pattern, the "best" thing to do depends entirely on what everyone else is doing.

One thought experiment from biology captures this perfectly: **Hawks and Doves**.

Imagine a population fighting over food, a piece of meat on the ground. In this world, there are two strategies (not two species, just two ways to play the game):

1. **The Hawk**: Aggressive and relentless. A Hawk will fight until it is either seriously injured or its opponent is dead. It never backs down.
2. **The Dove**: Not peaceful in a moral sense, but a master of posturing. A Dove will puff its feathers, hiss, and try to intimidate. But the second the opponent attacks, it retreats. It values its skin more than the meat.

Watch how the **Competitors** dictate the value of each action:

- **Hawk vs. Hawk**: They fight until one is horribly injured. The cost usually exceeds the value of the meat. A losing game for both.
- **Hawk vs. Dove**: The Hawk gets the meat for free. The Dove retreats with zero meat and no injuries.
- **Dove vs. Dove**: Long posturing, some wasted energy, eventually a split or a retreat. No one gets hurt.

In a world of **Pure Doves**, being a Dove is great. Everyone is polite and shares the meat. But this is an "Evolutionary Vacuum." A single **Hawk** mutant becomes a god — winning every fight without injury because Doves always run. It eats better, reproduces faster, and soon floods the population.

But a world of **Pure Hawks** is a nightmare. Everyone is constantly bleeding. The cost of injury is so high the average bird is worse off than if there were no meat at all. In that world, a **Dove** mutant wins by default: it loses every fight, but it *never gets injured*. Zero beats a negative score.

The Dove thrives in a world of Hawks. The Hawk thrives in a world of Doves.

The Value Function isn't tied to the meat; it's tied to the **Population**. The "best" strategy is invisible until you know who else is on the field. The system oscillates until it reaches an equilibrium where Hawks and Doves are equally fit.

You are a competitor in every system you participate in. The same forces play out in markets, careers, and industries every day.

The history of video games is a perfect laboratory for watching how competitors sculpt a value function.

Games began in **Arcades**. More players than machines. Owners needed rotation — play, lose, move so the next person could insert a coin.

This environment selected for **Difficulty**. *Pac-Man*, *Donkey Kong*, brutally hard by design. No save systems. No deep narratives. The Value Function rewarded games that could kill you in three minutes while making you feel like you *almost* won.

Developers weren't sadists. They were trying to survive the arcade market. A game too easy wouldn't make enough coins per hour. The competition forced a Hardcore meta. If you ever wondered why old games were so absurdly difficult, now you know.

Then the Track changed. Consoles and PCs arrived in our homes.

Now you paid once for the box. Rotation didn't matter. Lose too much, you'd get frustrated and skip the sequel. The Value Function shifted toward **Engagement** and **Narrative**. Games got easier. Added saves. Added long stories. The meta moved to shelf space, branding, and box art.

Then came the Internet and the **Mobile Revolution**.

The answer isn't the device's power. It's the **Collective Behavior** of the competitors.

Early digital was a wild frontier. Post a game on Facebook in 2009 and you could message all your friends for free. Zero marketing budget, millions of potential players. Simple, artistic experiments could survive because they didn't need to pay for eyes.

Then the Internet grew crowded. Organic reach began to die. The game changed.

Imagine two games of similar quality. One costs $3. The other is free. To a user scrolling through an app store, the free game has zero friction to entry. The $3 barrier feels like a wall; a "Free" button feels like an open door. Naturally, the free games reached a much larger audience. "Paid" games on mobile began to die because they couldn't compete with the lure of $0.

But developers still have to pay rent.

A free game with no revenue cannot survive. So developers started testing **In-App Purchases** — not predatory at first, just small upgrades to keep the lights on.

But once you have IAP, you have a new variable: **Revenue Per User**. That's where the competitive loop turns into a trap.

If your free game starts making $1 per player, you suddenly have a weapon. You can afford to spend money on **Marketing**. You start bidding for ads on Facebook or Google to find new users.

If my game makes $5 per player and yours makes $1, I can bid $2 per new user and still profit. You can't. I outbid you for every eyeball on the internet. Your game ends up in the app store graveyard.

As the market saturated, marketing became the most expensive part of making a game. To stay visible, you had to earn more from each user than you spent acquiring them.

This pressure forced monetization into increasingly extreme forms. Simple IAPs became **Gacha and Skinner Box** mechanics. Developers stopped targeting the average player and started hunting **Whales** — the 1% who spend thousands. The 99% of free players became just content to keep the servers populated. And to survive rising bids, many resorted to **Fake Ads**: showing non-existent gameplay to lower cost per click.

The industry didn't choose to be predatory. It was optimized into a corner. The cost of visibility rose so high that only the most aggressive monetizers could afford to stay. Today the mobile market is nearly impossible to break into honestly. The most fit organisms are the ones most efficient at converting players into revenue, then turning that revenue back into ads.

### The Mirror of the Field

The competitors define "The Meta." They find the loopholes. They push the edges.

On **Steam**, the Track evolved differently. The PC market matured before free-to-play and digital advertising existed. By the time those tools arrived, the audience was already formed: niche, paying, skeptical of anything that smelled like mobile. User reviews punish predatory design publicly. Refund policies raise the cost of shipping low-quality releases.

Developers on Steam aren't morally superior. They inherited a different Track. Mobile found a massive casual audience before expectations formed. Steam found a smaller, self-selecting audience that knew what it valued. Same tools, same internet, two very different equilibria.

But in any system where competition is fierce and feedback is fast, competitors will eventually optimize out all slack — forcing the system toward the most extreme version of its Value Function. Not a conspiracy. The collective behavior of millions of actors, all trying to survive the same Pattern.

You can't redesign a machine you haven't diagnosed.
