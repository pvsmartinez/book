## Chapter 25: Systemic Drift

The Pendulum swings. Hawks and Doves. Baggy and skinny. The system oscillates, but it oscillates around a center. The Value Function stays the same. The Rules stay the same. Only the mix of competitors shifts.

But what happens when the center itself starts moving?

We've already seen a glimpse of this. Think back to Delhi and the cobras. The administrators created a bounty: bring in a dead cobra, collect a reward. The goal was fewer cobras. The metric was skins collected. At first, the two aligned perfectly. Hunters went into the streets, and the cobra population dropped.

But the Pattern kept running. And now we have the language to see exactly why.

The first wave of hunters did the hard work. They tracked wild cobras through alleys and drains, risked bites, and collected their reward. Some hunters got lucky and stumbled onto a nest of hatchlings. Others trekked for hours to find a single large snake. Both were paid the same. That small asymmetry was a signal. The competitors of yesterday taught the competitors of today what the system actually rewarded, and it wasn't effort or danger. It was skins. Just skins.

So the next round of "hunters" adapted. Someone noticed that a pregnant cobra could produce dozens of offspring. Someone else realized that a backyard pen was safer than a midnight expedition. Each iteration refined the strategy. Within a few cycles, the most successful cobra suppliers weren't hunters at all. They were farmers. The metric kept climbing. The officials celebrated. Meanwhile, the actual cobra population was growing.

This is recursion doing what recursion does. Each round of results reshapes the next round of strategies. The proxy decoupled from the purpose not in a single leap, but through the slow, compounding logic of adaptation. When the British finally canceled the bounty, the breeders released their now-worthless stock. More cobras than before.

That was a small-scale version, playing out over a few years. But the same process can reshape entire industries over decades, and entire civilizations over centuries. Not a swing that returns to center. A slow migration of the system's entire purpose, driven by the same feedback loops that were supposed to serve it.

This is **systemic drift**.

### The Startup Machine

New ideas can generate enormous returns. A pharmaceutical breakthrough, a new kind of semiconductor, a way to connect millions of computers: these aren't incremental improvements. They're the kind of bets that, when they work, reshape entire industries. And as recent decades have shown, the returns on those bets can be staggering.

But banks can't make those bets. The way banking works (take deposits, lend conservatively, earn interest on the spread) doesn't allow for funding something with a 90% chance of failure. A bank that invested its depositors' money in ten unproven startups would be irresponsible. Banks are designed for predictable returns on predictable ventures: a mortgage, a car loan, a bakery expanding to a second location.

So there was a gap. Enormous potential on one side. Capital that couldn't reach it on the other side.

Venture capital filled that gap. The model was built on the mathematical reality known as the Power Law. A VC fund might invest in a hundred companies. They expect ninety to fail. Nine might return their money. But one (just one) could return a hundred times the initial investment. That single success compensates for every failure and generates profits for the entire fund.

Think of it like planting a forest. You scatter a thousand seeds knowing that squirrels, drought, and shade will kill most of them. You only need one to grow into a giant oak.

In the early days, this worked exactly as intended. In 1976, a 28-year-old venture capitalist named Robert Swanson cold-called a biochemist named Herbert Boyer. Boyer had recently co-invented a technique to splice DNA from one organism into another. Swanson thought this could become a business. They each contributed $500 and persuaded Kleiner Perkins, one of Silicon Valley's first venture firms, to back them. With that capital, they hired a small team, rented a laboratory, and began attempting to manufacture human proteins in engineered bacteria.

The company was called Genentech. Within two years, they produced synthetic human insulin, the first genetically engineered pharmaceutical. Four years after founding, Genentech went public. The company, which began with two people and $1,000, was eventually acquired by Roche for $46.8 billion.

This was the model at its best. An investor spots a breakthrough idea too risky for any bank. They fund the prototype. A small team builds a first product, takes it to market, and finds customers willing to pay for it. The Value Function was clear and grounded: build something that works, sell it to people who need it, share the profits.

At this point, venture capital was a handful of firms making a handful of bets. The **environment** was limited (local markets and physical distribution). The **competitors** were few (a dozen funds, mostly in California). And the **rules** were simple: a company succeeded when it turned a profit.

Then the environment shifted.

The internet unlocked global distribution. Suddenly, a single product could reach not thousands of customers but billions. And investors noticed something important: in networked markets, the biggest player tends to capture everything. If you're the leading search engine or the dominant e-commerce platform, second place might as well be last.

This made the new strategy rational. Don't worry about profit yet. Grow. Fast. Grab users. Lock in the network. Amazon lost money for years. Investors didn't care. Whoever owned the customer relationship would eventually own the market. And when those bets paid off (spectacularly, in the case of Amazon, Google, and Facebook), they proved something that hadn't been possible before the internet: a company could bleed cash for years, even a decade, and still become one of the most valuable enterprises in history.

That proof changed the rules. Revenue was no longer coupled with success. Success no longer meant selling a product at a profit. It meant capturing the network.

The **environment** had shifted. And with it, the Value Function moved.

Then the **competitors** multiplied.

The spectacular returns attracted imitation. What had been a handful of firms in California became an industry of thousands. Pension funds, university endowments, and sovereign wealth funds all wanted a piece of the returns. Capital flooded in. Fund sizes ballooned. And this mattered enormously, because a handful of funds making bets on startups is an experiment. Thousands of funds making bets on startups is an *ecosystem*, one large enough to sustain its own internal market. A VC firm no longer needed its company to go public or get acquired by a corporation to cash out. It could sell its shares to a *later-stage VC firm*. And that later-stage firm could sell to an even later one. The exit was no longer the customer. The exit was the next investor.

With that crowding, the **rules** shifted.

With so many investors competing, valuations inflated. And founders noticed something critical: you didn't need to please the *customer* to survive. You needed to please the *investor*. Show the right growth charts. Master the pitch deck. Raise the next round at a higher valuation.

The pattern is selected accordingly. Founders who were world-class at fundraising raised the most money. Founders who were world-class at building products often couldn't compete for attention. The system began producing companies that had never turned a profit but were valued in the billions, because each round of investors only needed to convince the *next* round that the story was getting better.

The loop closed on itself. Early investors didn't need the company ever to make money. They just needed a later investor to buy their shares at a higher price. The entire chain could sustain itself as long as the narrative held and new capital continued to flow.

All three levers of the Value Function shifted. The environment changed (the internet made global-scale communication possible). The **competitors** changed (a handful of firms became an industry of thousands, creating an ecosystem of buyers and sellers). The **rules** changed (revenue decoupled from success, then success decoupled from customers entirely).

The system traveled from:
1. "Build something people want."
2. "Get as many users as possible."
3. "Raise the next round at a higher valuation."

Each step made sense from inside the system. Each step was a rational response to the selection pressures of its moment. But zoom out across five decades, and the original intention (fund breakthrough innovations, let customers decide which ones matter) has drifted far from what the system now optimizes for.

Does venture capital still fund breakthroughs? Absolutely. Some of the most important technologies of the last decade emerged from venture-backed companies. But the system also produces billion-dollar valuations attached to little more than a narrative and a pitch deck. It still generates innovation, but perhaps not sustainable innovation, because the feedback loop no longer requires profit. The drift doesn't make the system better or worse in an absolute sense. It makes it *different*. Pointed somewhere new. And that's the nature of systemic drift: not a failure, but a quiet migration that nobody voted for and everyone participated in.

### From Competition to Consolidation

There is another form of systemic drift, older than venture capital by centuries, and arguably more consequential. It's the drift that occurs when a market full of many small competitors slowly consolidates into one dominated by a few large firms, and the rules of the system shift to protect the winners rather than reward the best.

We saw earlier that Adam Smith's invisible hand is merely a name for the Pattern operating within markets. Many players are competing, iterating, and adapting. When the conditions are right (lots of competitors, low barriers to entry, nobody powerful enough to rewrite the rules), the Pattern genuinely produces good outcomes. Prices drop. Quality rises. The system breathes.

But those conditions don't last forever. And Venice is the clearest example of why.

Around the year 1000, Venice was arguably the most prosperous and entrepreneurial society in the world. Built on water with no farmland, it couldn't be ruled by traditional lords who derived power from land. Instead, it was ruled by merchants. And merchants understood something that aristocrats often didn't: wealth had to be *created*, not just inherited.

Venice's engine was a contract called the **Commenda**.

Here's how it worked. A young, ambitious trader with no capital could partner with a wealthy merchant. The trader would take a ship across the Mediterranean to buy and sell goods: spices from the East, glass from Murano, silk from Constantinople. If the voyage succeeded, the trader kept a significant share of the profits, typically a quarter to a third. If it failed, the investor bore the financial loss.

This was Venice's version of venture capital. And like venture capital, it was built on the Power Law. Most voyages were modest. Some failed. But the occasional spectacular success (a new trade route, a monopoly on a particular good) generated enough wealth to reshape a family's fortune in a single generation.

The Commenda did something radical for its time: it separated talent from wealth. A poor kid from the dockyards could become one of the richest people in the city within a few years, purely on ability and nerve. Economists would later call this kind of arrangement an **inclusive institution**: a set of rules designed to keep the playing field open, reward risk-taking, and let new entrants compete on merit. Venice's entire system was built on this principle. Its laws promoted trade, its contracts distributed risk, and its government (at least in the early centuries) remained accessible to successful merchants regardless of birth.

And Venice thrived. By the thirteenth century, it was the richest, most cosmopolitan city in Europe. Its fleet dominated the Mediterranean trade. Its merchants were the most sophisticated financiers in the world. The invisible hand, working inside these inclusive rules, produced exactly what you'd hope: widespread prosperity driven by competition and talent.

But the Pattern never stops. The winners compounded.

The successful merchants became wealthy. The wealthy entered politics. The politically powerful viewed the next generation of ambitious young traders not as the future of Venice but as a threat to their own position.

In 1297, they triggered **La Serrata** ("The Closing").

They locked the **Golden Book**, the registry of families allowed to participate in government. They restricted and eventually banned the Commenda. They enacted laws that made it more difficult for new families to enter trade. Step by step, they rewrote the rules of the system that had made them rich.

The institutions that had been inclusive became **extractive**: closed to outsiders, designed to protect insiders, structured to preserve inherited advantage. The rules no longer rewarded the best traders. They rewarded the best-connected families. The invisible hand was still there, still selecting, but now it was selecting inside a closed system. Within a closed system, competition ceases and consolidation begins.

Venice didn't collapse because of a war, a plague, or a stronger rival. It drifted. Generation by generation, the rules shifted until the engine of global commerce became a beautiful, frozen artifact.

This is the drift that the invisible hand, on its own, cannot correct. In a young market with many small players, the Pattern drives competition, and competition drives quality. But as winners compound, they gain the power to rewrite the rules in their favor. The market matures from open to consolidated. The institutions shift from inclusive to extractive. And the invisible hand continues to select, but now it selects for companies (or families, or factions) that are best at *preventing* competition, not winning it.

Assuming the hand will always self-correct is akin to assuming the pendulum will always swing back to its center. It usually does. But if someone welds it in place (through regulatory capture, monopoly, or institutional lock-in), the correction never comes.

Systemic drift is why markets need maintenance. Not because the mechanism is broken, but because the mechanism is too good at adapting to whatever rules exist, including rules that have drifted far from the ones that made it useful.

### The Slow Fade

Drift is why "fixing" a system is so hard. You aren't fighting a bug. You're fighting the fact that every actor in the system has already adapted to the drifted rules.

The venture capitalists built their careers around the valuation game. The Venetian aristocrats built their identities around the Golden Book. The monopolist built its moat. Everyone inside the system is locally rational. Everyone is doing exactly what the current Value Function rewards. The original intention is a ghost that no one remembers, and even if they did, returning to it would entail dismantling the structures on which everyone now depends.

Every system drifts. I've watched it happen in companies I've worked at, in communities I've been part of, in habits I didn't notice changing until they'd already changed. The question for the designer isn't how to prevent it. Drift is a feature of any living system, not a defect. The question is whether you've built in a way to notice it. Because by the time a system arrives at its new destination, everyone inside will swear that's where it was always headed.
