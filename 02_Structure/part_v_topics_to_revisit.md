## From Part IV Review Sessions (Feb 2026)

### 1. Luck as a Design Lever
**Seed:** [[luck_as_a_design_lever]]
**Summary:** Luck (variance) is a dial designers can turn. High-luck systems are inclusive but volatile. Low-luck systems reward mastery but entrench winners. Every real-world system (job markets, schools, funding) has this dial whether designers acknowledge it or not. Explore: how do you decide where to set it? What are the trade-offs?

### 2. Acknowledging the Head Start (Designing Around Imbalance)
**Source:** Ch 19 review
**Summary:** The chess insight: you can't always remove the imbalance, but you can design rules *around* it (alternating sides, draws favoring Black). Expand on practical strategies for compensating for uneven starting positions without pretending the system is fair.

### 3. The ETF Feedback Loop as a Design Failure
**Source:** Ch 19 review
**Summary:** ETFs created an unintended self-reinforcing loop (big companies get bigger automatically). Nobody designed it on purpose; it emerged from a simple rule meeting scale. Case study for: how do you detect and manage emergent feedback loops?

### 4. The Designer's Acknowledgment (Part IV endings pattern)
**Source:** Style note added Feb 2026
**Summary:** Each Part IV chapter ends with a subtle nod toward the designer's perspective. Part V is where that seed blooms into actual tools and strategies. Make sure Part V picks up these threads explicitly.

## From Seeds (Already Written)

### 5. Rock-Paper-Scissors Dynamics
**Seed:** [[rock_paper_scissors_dynamics]]
**Summary:** Designing for healthy counter-dynamics (oscillation) rather than trying to pick a single "best" strategy. Tanks > Assassins > DPS > Tanks. Nash equilibrium as a design goal. Applies to fashion, economics, politics.

### 6. The System Designer Mindset (Core Seed)
**Seed:** [[the_system_designer_mindset]]
**Summary:** The central Part V philosophy. Mindset shift from "fight the players" to "redesign the game." Dual approach: symptom management (Tylenol) + system redesign (Antibiotics). Failed Nerf vs. Successful Rework. The favela case study.

### 7. The Game Designer's Toolkit
**Seed:** [[the_game_designer_role]]
**Summary:** Balance patching, economy management (Faucets & Sinks), creating environments that guide behavior. "Patch the code, not the player." Direct material for Ch 30 and Ch 32.

### 8. Iterated Games and Cooperation (incl. Tit for Tat)
**Seed:** [[iterated_games_and_cooperation]]
**Removed from:** Ch 23 (The Pendulum) during Feb 2026 review. Full Tit for Tat / Axelrod tournament write-up was drafted but didn't fit the Pendulum's theme. Content is ready to reuse.
**Summary:** Four design levers for encouraging cooperation: make the game repeat, make betrayal visible, make the future matter, reduce noise. Tit for Tat as the dominant strategy in iterated games. Shadow of the Future. Dawkins' point that cooperation is what selfish genes produce when the game repeats. Explicitly tagged as Part V toolkit content. Bridges to "Patching the Code."

### 9. Symptoms vs. Systems (The Dual Approach)
**Seed:** [[symptoms_vs_systems_the_dual_approach]]
**Summary:** You need both short-term symptom management and long-term system redesign. Direct material for Ch 28 (The Hydra) and Part V's overall framework.

### 10. Shifting Value Functions
**Seed:** [[shifting_value_functions]]
**Summary:** When the value function drifts to extremes, the designer must introduce counter-forces. You can't ask agents to "be better"; you must edit the environment.

### 11. The Levers Concept
**Seed:** [[the_levers_concept]]
**Summary:** The three levers any system designer can pull: Volume, Variance, Latency. Foundational toolkit content.

### 12. Game Theory as a Modeling Tool
**Seed:** [[game_theory]]
**Summary:** Game theory as a practical tool for the system designer (the "Gardener"). Useful for modeling interactions before designing interventions.

### 17. The Lock-in Breaker (Switching Cost Math)
**Source:** Removed interlude from Part IV (27b_interlude_the_time_machine.md)
**Summary:** Switching costs are a one-time fee; staying locked in is a recurring tax that compounds forever. The Excel Trap case study: staying on a broken spreadsheet costs 500+ hours over 5 years; migrating to a proper system costs 80 hours once. Application: identify where you're paying a recurring tax to avoid a one-time switch, calculate the 10-year cost, and break the lock-in. Fits naturally in a Part V chapter about escaping traps or designing your own rules.

### 17. The Lock-In Problem (Why Fixes Are Hard)
**Seed:** [[the_trap_lock_in]]
**Summary:** Why we can't just "fix" education or other entrenched systems. The entire economy is built around the credential signal. Part V needs to acknowledge that patching has constraints.

### 18. The Blind Spot (What the Designer Must Correct)
**Seed:** [[the_blind_spot_of_selection]]
**Summary:** The system's value function has a narrow window. The designer must consciously widen it. Selection is a filter, not an omniscient optimizer.
